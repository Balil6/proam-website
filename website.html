The Pro.am philosophy

Excellence in training.
Without giving up
everything else.

Pro.am exists for one type of person. Someone who wants to pursue excellence in their training, not just show up and go through the motions, but genuinely optimise, progress, and perform at the highest level they are capable of.

That person also has a full time job. A schedule that doesn't bend easily. Responsibilities that come first. The ambition is professional. The circumstances are not.

Most coaching programmes are built for people who have unlimited time, unlimited recovery, and no life outside the gym. That is not who we work with. We take the guesswork out of training for people who cannot afford to waste a session.

Every programme we build is fully optimised, the right exercises, the right volume, the right progression, so that every hour you spend training counts. No filler. No generic plans. No compromises on quality.

You should not have to choose between training seriously and living fully.

That is the whole point. Pro.am is built on the belief that with the right programme and the right coaching, you can reach a level of performance that most people would call professional, around a full time job and full time responsibilities.

What this means in practice

  • — Programmes built around your schedule, not an ideal one
  • — Every session has a purpose, nothing is filler
  • — Progressive overload built in from week one
  • — Strength and endurance treated as complementary, not competing
  • — No guesswork, you know exactly what to do and why

About Will

I built this because
I needed it.

For a long time I could not find the balance between getting stronger and training for endurance events. When one improved the other suffered. Every programme I tried treated them as opposing goals.

Having played rugby at a national and semi-professional level as a prop, strength had always come first for me. When my career was cut short by injury and I started taking endurance seriously I had to rethink how I trained entirely. Programming May 2025

Why most hybrid programmes fail before week four

The problem is not commitment. It is not even the training. Most hybrid programmes are built by people who understand either strength or endurance. Rarely both.

If you have ever tried to run and lift seriously at the same time, you will know the feeling. The first two weeks go well. You are fresh, motivated, and the programme feels manageable. Then week three arrives and something starts to slip.

Usually it is the running. The legs never quite recover between sessions. Or it is the lifting. The numbers stop moving because your body is always carrying fatigue from the cardio. Either way, by week four most people have quietly dropped one of the two.

The problem is not you

Most hybrid programmes are written by coaches who specialise in one discipline. A strength coach who adds some cardio. An endurance coach who throws in a few lifting sessions. The result is a programme that does not understand how the two types of training interact with each other.

Strength training and endurance training create different demands on your body. When you lift heavy, you are creating mechanical stress on the muscles. When you run, you are creating metabolic stress on the same system. Stack them without thinking about the order, the volume, and the recovery, and they compete directly with each other.

What actually works

The fix is not complicated but it requires the programme to be built with both goals in mind from the start. Not strength first with some cardio bolted on. Not an endurance programme with a gym day added in. Both, designed together, sequenced properly.

  • Hard strength sessions and hard cardio sessions should not sit back to back
  • Volume needs to be managed across the week as a whole, not session by session
  • The weeks need to be periodised so the body can absorb both types of stress
  • Recovery is not optional. It is where the adaptation happens.

The compromise people talk about between strength and endurance does not have to exist. It is a programming problem, not a physiological one.

That is the foundation Pro.am is built on. If you want to see what a programme built with both goals in mind actually looks like, start with the free week.

Progressive Overload April 2025

The three ways to progress. Most people only use one.

Adding weight to the bar is the most obvious form of progressive overload. But it is not the only one, and when you are training around a busy schedule it is often not the most practical.

Progressive overload is the principle that your training needs to become harder over time for your body to keep adapting. Without it, you stop making progress. Most people understand this in theory. In practice, most people interpret it as one thing: add more weight.

That works. But it has limits. You cannot add weight to every lift every week indefinitely. At some point, and usually sooner than people expect, the numbers stop going up on that timeline.

The three methods

Add weight. Same reps, heavier bar. The most direct form of overload. When your technique is solid and you hit the top of your rep range cleanly, add weight next session.

Add reps. Same weight, one or two more reps than last time. This is the method to use when you are not quite ready to jump up in weight. It is underrated and often more appropriate, particularly later in a training block.

Add exercises. Same weight and reps on each lift, more total work in the session. This is volume cycling, and it is the method most people ignore completely. Starting a programme with fewer exercises and building up across the weeks means you are doing more total work by week five than you were in week one. That is progress, even when the individual lift numbers have not moved.

Which one to use

The answer depends on where you are in the programme and how your body is responding. A well-built programme will tell you. The ones that do not are leaving progress on the table.

Something must go up every week. Weight, reps, or exercises. Pick one. If none of the three are moving, something needs to change.

Mindset March 2025

You do not need more time. You need a better programme.

Time is the reason most people give for not training as seriously as they want to. It is rarely the real problem.

Ask someone why their training is not where they want it to be and the answer is almost always the same. Not enough time. Too busy. Work gets in the way. The schedule does not allow for it.

There is some truth in this. Time is finite and training does take time. But most people who say they do not have enough time are not actually short on time. They are short on a programme that fits the time they have.

The real problem

Generic programmes are built around ideal conditions. Four or five days a week, an hour per session, no other physical demands on the body. For most people training around full time responsibilities, those conditions do not exist.

When the programme does not fit your life, you do not follow the programme. You skip sessions. You do half of it. You start again on Monday for the third time this month. It is not a motivation problem. It is a mismatch between the programme and the person following it.

What a programme built for your life looks like

  • It is built around the days and hours you actually have, not an ideal week
  • Every session has a clear purpose so nothing feels like filler
  • The volume is appropriate for your recovery capacity, not a professional athlete
  • Missing one session does not break the whole structure

Three well-designed sessions a week will outperform five mediocre ones almost every time. The goal is not more training. It is better training, fitted properly to the life around it.

The people I work with are not short on commitment. They are short on a programme that was built for them.
